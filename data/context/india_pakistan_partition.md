# India/Pakistan: The Partition and Its Inheritance
<!-- Source: GeoPulse authored context | Updated: 2026-03 -->

## The Partition (1947)

British India was divided into two independent dominions — India and Pakistan — in August 1947. The Radcliffe Line, the border between them, was drawn by Cyril Radcliffe, a British lawyer who had never previously visited India, in approximately five weeks, based largely on maps and census data. The line bisected Punjab and Bengal — two of the subcontinent's most densely populated and ethnically mixed regions.

The consequences were immediate and catastrophic:
- Estimated 10–20 million people displaced in the largest forced migration in history
- Estimated 200,000–2,000,000 killed in communal violence (numbers remain contested)
- The displacement was directional: Muslims moved to Pakistan (East and West), Hindus and Sikhs moved to India — but the lines were never clean

The speed and violence of Partition left wounds that have not healed across three generations. Families were divided. Centuries-old neighborhoods were destroyed. The trauma is a living presence in South Asian literature, culture, and politics.

## The Two-Nation Theory

Pakistan was founded on the two-nation theory: that Hindus and Muslims are not merely different religious communities within a shared civilization but constitute two distinct nations, each requiring a separate state. This theory, associated primarily with Muhammad Ali Jinnah, was the political logic of Partition.

India was founded on a different premise: that it would be a secular republic in which religious identity would not determine citizenship or political belonging. Jawaharlal Nehru's vision of a post-religious, socialist, modernizing India was the explicit counter to the two-nation theory.

Both premises have been under pressure ever since. Pakistan's Islamic identity has intensified and fragmented (Sunni/Shia tensions, Baloch separatism, the Ahmadiyya question). India's secular nationalism has been progressively displaced by Hindu nationalist (Hindutva) politics, culminating in the BJP's dominance under Narendra Modi since 2014. The Citizenship Amendment Act (2019) — which offered expedited citizenship to persecuted minorities from Afghanistan, Bangladesh, and Pakistan, but excluded Muslims — was read by critics as the formal arrival of the two-nation theory inside India's own constitutional structure.

## Kashmir

Jammu & Kashmir was a princely state with a Muslim majority population ruled by a Hindu maharaja, Hari Singh. At Partition, princely states were given the choice to accede to either India or Pakistan. Singh delayed, hoping perhaps for independence. In October 1947, Pakistani tribal militias (backed by the Pakistani military) invaded. Singh signed the Instrument of Accession to India in exchange for military assistance, with the understanding that the accession would be confirmed by plebiscite once order was restored.

The plebiscite never happened. Three wars have been fought over Kashmir (1947–48, 1965, 1999). The Line of Control (LoC) — the de facto border — is not an internationally recognized boundary. India administers Jammu & Kashmir (since 2019, as a Union Territory following revocation of Article 370). Pakistan administers Azad Kashmir and Gilgit-Baltistan. China administers the Aksai Chin (since 1962). The dispute is tripartite.

India's position: accession was legal and final; Pakistan's interference is cross-border terrorism; Article 370 revocation is a domestic constitutional matter. Pakistan's position: the plebiscite was promised and never held; the Muslim majority population's right to self-determination has been denied; Indian atrocities in the Valley are systematic. Kashmiri views are not monolithic — there are pro-India, pro-Pakistan, and pro-independence factions, and suppression of all political voices has made credible polling impossible for decades.

## The Nuclear Dimension

India tested nuclear weapons in 1974 ("peaceful nuclear explosion") and openly in 1998. Pakistan tested in response in 1998. Both are nuclear-armed states not party to the Non-Proliferation Treaty. Neither has ratified the Comprehensive Test Ban Treaty.

They have no formal agreement on no-first-use with each other (India has a declared no-first-use policy; Pakistan explicitly does not, because its conventional forces are smaller and it uses nuclear threat as an equalizer). The Kargil War (1999) — the only war between two nuclear-armed states — was fought at a time when both had recently tested weapons, and nuclear signaling was a real factor in the escalation and de-escalation.

This remains one of the highest-probability nuclear conflict scenarios on Earth, combining: unresolved territorial dispute, domestic nationalist pressure on both sides, history of near-wars, Pakistani military doctrine that includes tactical nuclear weapons, and the absence of reliable crisis communication channels.

## The India-China-Pakistan Triangle

The Sino-Pakistani alliance (formalized in the 1963 border agreement, in which China recognized Pakistani control of parts of Kashmir that Pakistan had ceded) was forged partly as a counterweight to India after the 1962 Sino-Indian War (in which China routed Indian forces in the Himalayas, a national trauma India has never fully processed publicly). The China-Pakistan Economic Corridor (CPEC), a flagship BRI project, runs through Pakistani-administered Kashmir — which India considers its sovereign territory, making CPEC an additional point of friction in the India-China relationship.

India's "non-alignment" tradition (now called "strategic autonomy") — its refusal to join either Cold War bloc and its continuing resistance to formal alliance with the United States — is shaped partly by the desire to maintain freedom of action in this triangle.

## What This Means for Briefings

- India-Pakistan crises escalate rapidly and unpredictably. The 2019 Pulwama attack → Balakot airstrike → aerial engagement sequence moved from terrorist attack to near-war in days.
- Domestic politics in both states push toward escalation: in India, Hindu nationalist mobilization around "muscular nationalism"; in Pakistan, the military's institutional interest in the Kashmir dispute as a justification for its political dominance.
- Any India-Pakistan event should be read through the nuclear lens. The risk is real, not theoretical.
- India's relationship with the US, Russia, and China cannot be understood without the Pakistan-Kashmir-1962 layer.
