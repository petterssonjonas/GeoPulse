# China: Historical Frame
<!-- Source: GeoPulse authored context | Updated: 2026-03 -->

## The Century of Humiliation (百年屈辱, 1839–1949)

China was the world's largest economy as recently as 1820 (roughly 30% of global GDP). In less than a century, it was subjected to serial military defeats, forced to sign over 700 "unequal treaties," lost control of its ports and tariffs to foreign powers, watched foreign troops march into Beijing twice (1860, 1900), ceded Taiwan to Japan in 1895, and was partially occupied by Japan from 1931–1945.

The humiliations came from every direction:
- **Britain:** Opium Wars (1839–42, 1856–60); forced opium trade, Hong Kong cession, treaty ports
- **France:** Tonkin campaigns, Sino-French War (1884–85), Indochina
- **Germany:** Jiaozhou Bay, sphere of influence in Shandong
- **Russia:** territorial losses in Manchuria and Central Asia, Trans-Siberian sphere of influence
- **Japan:** First Sino-Japanese War (1894–95), Taiwan cession, Twenty-One Demands (1915), Manchuria (1931), full-scale invasion (1937)
- **United States:** Open Door Policy (treating China as a market to be shared among powers), exclusion laws targeting Chinese immigrants

The People's Republic, founded 1949, is explicitly narrated as ending this period. "Standing up" (站起来) is the foundational act.

## National Rejuvenation and Xi Jinping

Xi Jinping's "Chinese Dream" (中国梦) of "the great rejuvenation of the Chinese nation" (中华民族伟大复兴) is the successor frame. The target date — 2049, the centenary of the PRC — is the endpoint at which China is to have fully restored what was taken.

This is not purely propaganda. It resonates across the political spectrum inside China, including among people who are critical of the CCP on other grounds. The emotional substrate is real.

## Taiwan

Taiwan was ceded to Japan in the Treaty of Shimonoseki (1895) — the price of losing the First Sino-Japanese War. It was returned to the Republic of China in 1945, then separated from mainland control after the Communist victory in 1949 when the ROC government retreated there.

In the Chinese national frame, Taiwan is an open wound of the humiliation era that has not been healed. It is not a border dispute or an irredentist claim; it is the last unresolved piece of national restoration. This is why no Chinese leader — not even one who wanted to — could concede Taiwan's permanent independence and survive politically.

The Western frame — Taiwan as a de facto independent democracy whose 23 million people have the right to self-determination — is coherent and defensible. Both frames are held simultaneously by thoughtful people and cannot be wished away.

## Sovereignty and Non-Interference

The legal principle of sovereignty and non-interference in internal affairs carries a weight in Chinese foreign policy that is not cynical deflection. It maps directly onto the memory of 150 years in which foreign powers used everything from moral justification (civilizing mission, human rights) to economic pressure to military force to override Chinese governance. When China invokes "non-interference," it is invoking a principle it wishes had existed when Britain was forcing opium through gunboat diplomacy.

This does not mean every use of the non-interference principle is in good faith. But the principle has a historical substrate that purely Western-framed analysis consistently underweights.

## Belt and Road (一带一路)

The BRI is simultaneously a genuine infrastructure initiative, an instrument of economic statecraft, and a civilizational signal. The name invokes the ancient Silk Road — a period in which China was the dominant civilizational power at the center of Eurasian trade. The signal is: we are returning to our natural position.

Critics (debt trap diplomacy, opaque contracts, strategic port access) and supporters (genuine infrastructure gap-filling in the Global South, no political conditionality) both have evidence. The frame matters: the BRI is not just about economics.
