# Middle East: Ottoman Dissolution and the Mandate State System
<!-- Source: GeoPulse authored context | Updated: 2026-03 -->

## The Ottoman Dissolution (1908–1923)

The Ottoman Empire — which at its peak ruled Anatolia, the Levant, Mesopotamia, Arabia, Egypt, and large parts of North Africa and Southeast Europe — had been contracting since the 18th century under pressure from European powers, nationalist movements, and internal reform failures. Its entry into World War I on the German side accelerated the collapse.

By 1923, the empire was formally dissolved, succeeded by the Republic of Turkey under Mustafa Kemal (Atatürk). The Arab territories were divided between Britain and France under League of Nations mandates.

The collapse was not just political. The Ottoman system had provided a multi-confessional legal and social framework — imperfect, hierarchical, but functioning — in which Jews, Christians, and Muslims lived in defined but coexisting arrangements for four centuries. Its destruction, and its replacement with European-style nation-states, required that religious and ethnic diversity be resolved into majority/minority frameworks that the new states often handled through violence or expulsion.

## Sykes-Picot and the Mandate System (1916–1923)

The Sykes-Picot Agreement (May 1916), negotiated secretly between British diplomat Mark Sykes and French diplomat François Georges-Picot, divided the anticipated post-Ottoman Arab territories into British and French spheres of influence. The lines were drawn on a map with a ruler, without reference to geography, water systems, tribal territories, or ethnic concentrations.

The resulting mandate states:
- **Syria and Lebanon** — French mandate. Lebanon was carved out as a separate state to give Maronite Christians a majority (it worked briefly; Christian demographic advantage has long since reversed).
- **Palestine** — British mandate. The Balfour Declaration (1917) promised a "national home for the Jewish people" while ostensibly not prejudicing "civil and religious rights of existing non-Jewish communities." Both halves of that promise proved impossible to honor simultaneously.
- **Iraq** — British mandate, then kingdom. Three Ottoman provinces (Mosul, Baghdad, Basra) were merged into a single state, joining Sunni Arabs, Shia Arabs, and Kurds under a Hashemite king with no historical connection to the territory.
- **Transjordan** — British mandate, carved partly from the Palestine mandate to give the Hashemite family a second throne after their ejection from Arabia by the Saudis.

The borders were not designed to create viable states. They were designed to serve British and French strategic and economic interests (oil, Suez, India route, Mediterranean influence).

## The Palestinian Question

The Balfour Declaration's promise was made without consulting the Arab population of Palestine (roughly 90% of the total at the time). Jewish immigration increased significantly under the mandate, triggering communal violence (1920, 1929, 1936–39 Arab Revolt). After World War II and the Holocaust, the pressure for a Jewish state was overwhelming.

The 1947 UN Partition Plan proposed dividing Palestine into Jewish and Arab states. Jewish leadership accepted it; Arab states and Palestinian leadership rejected it. Israel declared independence in 1948; Arab states invaded; Israel won and expanded beyond the partition lines. 700,000–750,000 Palestinians fled or were expelled — the Nakba ("catastrophe"), which is foundational to Palestinian national identity as the Partition is to South Asian identity.

The 1967 War added the West Bank, Gaza, Sinai, and Golan Heights to Israeli control. Sinai was returned to Egypt in the Camp David Accords (1979). Gaza and the West Bank remain the contested core of the unresolved question.

**Western consensus:** A two-state solution along roughly 1967 lines is the path to peace; Israeli settlement expansion is an obstacle; Palestinian leadership failures (corruption, division between Fatah and Hamas) are also obstacles. Israel has a right to security.

**Arab/Islamic consensus:** The 1948 displacement was ethnic cleansing; the occupation is illegal under international law; resistance, including armed resistance, is legitimate. Israel's existence within its current form is contested by significant portions of this consensus (though not all — several Arab states have normalized relations).

**Israeli right-wing/religious-nationalist frame:** Biblical/historical Jewish claim to the land is primary; the West Bank is "Judea and Samaria," not occupied territory; security requirements justify settlement and military control.

These are not equivalent in all respects — international law is reasonably clear on occupation — but all three are internally coherent and represent real constituencies. A briefing that presents only one is propaganda.

## Iraq and the Sectarian Trap

Iraq was always an artificial state. The 2003 US invasion destroyed the one mechanism — Baathist authoritarian control — that had suppressed its internal contradictions. The de-Baathification policy dismantled the state and military, releasing trained Sunni fighters into unemployment and resentment. The result was an insurgency, a civil war, the creation of Al-Qaeda in Iraq (AQI), and eventually ISIS.

ISIS explicitly named the abolition of Sykes-Picot as a founding act — declaring a "caliphate" that erased the Iraq-Syria border as its first territorial statement. The Islamic State was not simply a terrorist organization but a state-building project responding to the legitimacy vacuum of the mandate-era borders.

Iraq today is a weak state with a Shia-dominated government, Iranian influence through Shia militias, Kurdish semi-independence in the north, and ongoing Sunni alienation. The Sykes-Picot border problem has not been solved; it has been managed at enormous cost.

## Lebanon as Case Study

Lebanon was designed as a Christian-majority state in which French interests would have a reliable partner. Power-sharing (the National Pact, 1943) divided government by sect: Maronite president, Sunni prime minister, Shia speaker. This formula was outdated by the 1970s as Shia became the largest community. The 1975–1990 civil war, Israel's invasions (1978, 1982, 2006), the Palestinian presence, Syrian occupation, and Hezbollah's rise as a state-within-a-state have all been consequences of the original design failure.

Lebanon is the clearest example of what happens when borders create confessional majorities that are guaranteed to shift.

## What This Means for Briefings

- Middle Eastern conflicts almost never have purely contemporary causes. The Sykes-Picot inheritance — weak states, sectarian borders, legitimacy deficits — is always structural background.
- "Sectarian conflict" is usually also state-legitimacy conflict. When Sunni and Shia fight, they are fighting over which community controls a state whose borders they did not choose.
- Iran's regional influence (through Shia political movements in Iraq, Lebanon, Syria, Yemen, Bahrain) is not simply expansion; it is filling the vacuum left by weak mandate states and by the US-destroyed Iraqi state.
- The Palestinian question is not resolvable by Western consensus frameworks alone because the consensus is not shared by the parties.
