# Russia/Ukraine: Historical Frame
<!-- Source: GeoPulse authored context | Updated: 2026-03 -->

## The Varangian Rus and the Origin Dispute

The Kievan Rus (roughly 9th–13th century CE) was a federation of principalities centered on Kyiv, founded by Varangian (Norse) settlers who moved down the Dnieper river trading route from Scandinavia. The Rus elites converted to Orthodox Christianity from Byzantium in 988 CE under Vladimir the Great — an event both Russia and Ukraine regard as civilizationally foundational.

After the Mongol sack of Kyiv in 1240, the political center of Rus moved northeast, eventually settling in Muscovy. Muscovite princes adopted the title "Tsar of all Rus" and constructed a continuous lineage from Kyiv to Moscow.

**The Russian national tradition** reads this as: Kyiv was the cradle, Moscow is the continuity. Ukraine is not a separate civilization but a regional expression — "Little Russia" (Malorossiya) in 19th-century Russian imperial terminology. The word "Ukraine" (Ukraina) is often glossed as "borderland" in Russian-language discourse, implying periphery.

**The Ukrainian national tradition** — particularly the historiography of Mykhailo Hrushevsky (1866–1934) — argues the opposite: Kievan Rus was specifically the ancestor of Ukraine. Muscovy was a separate political development, heavily influenced by its 240 years under Mongol suzerainty, and its claim to Kyivan continuity is a constructed legitimation of later imperial expansion. Ukrainian language, culture, folk traditions, and the specific Cossack political tradition (the Hetmanate) constitute a distinct civilization.

Both traditions are real. Neither is simply wrong or simply right. They are rival frameworks for the same historical material, and the war is, in part, a war about which one is true.

## The Orthodox Church Dimension

The Russian Orthodox Church (Moscow Patriarchate) claimed jurisdiction over Ukrainian Orthodox Christians as part of its claim to be the legitimate heir of Kyivan Christianity. In 2018–2019, the Ecumenical Patriarch of Constantinople recognized a new Orthodox Church of Ukraine (OCU), independent of Moscow.

This was not a minor ecclesiastical dispute. It removed a major pillar of the "one people" theological claim and was understood in Moscow as a direct attack on the civilizational argument for Russian-Ukrainian unity. Patriarch Kirill of Moscow has framed the war in part as a religious war against this schism.

## Putin's Temporal Anchor

Putin's clearest statement of his historical frame is his July 2021 essay, "On the Historical Unity of Russians and Ukrainians." The core argument: Russians and Ukrainians are one people; Ukrainian national identity was artificially constructed, first by Austro-Hungarian policy (to weaken Russia), then by Lenin's nationalities policy (which gave Ukraine a fictitious "statehood" within the USSR). The post-1991 Ukrainian state is an illegitimate artifact of this history.

Putin's primary temporal anchor is **not** the Soviet Union, though he called its collapse "the greatest geopolitical catastrophe of the 20th century." His deeper anchor is pre-modern, Orthodox, and imperial — a civilizational continuity of "historical Russia" that predates modern state borders entirely.

This makes negotiated settlement structurally difficult. For Putin, the premise of Ukrainian sovereignty is the error. You cannot negotiate around a premise the other side regards as the problem.

## NATO Expansion

The Western consensus: sovereign states have the right to choose their alliances. NATO expansion reflects the choice of Eastern European states that experienced Soviet occupation and sought security guarantees. Russia has no legitimate veto over this.

The Russian (and some Western realist) frame: NATO expansion broke informal commitments made during German reunification negotiations; moving a military alliance to Russia's borders is an objective security threat regardless of stated intentions; great powers always maintain spheres of influence and Western behavior in Latin America (Monroe Doctrine) confirms this norm.

The evidence on informal 1990 commitments is genuinely contested among historians. The security-threat logic has some force — NATO infrastructure moving to Russia's border is not nothing — but it does not justify invasion under international law. Both frames need to be held.

## The 2014 Rupture

The Maidan revolution (2014) — triggered by Yanukovych's abrupt refusal to sign the EU Association Agreement under Russian pressure — removed a Russia-aligned government. In the Russian frame: a Western-engineered coup on Russia's border. In the Ukrainian frame: a popular uprising against a kleptocratic president who had just agreed to preserve his power by killing protesters. Both things contain true elements.

Russia's annexation of Crimea (March 2014) followed within weeks. Crimea had been part of the Russian SFSR until 1954, when Khrushchev transferred it to the Ukrainian SSR — an administrative move that became consequential when the USSR dissolved. Sevastopol hosts Russia's Black Sea Fleet. The annexation was illegal under international law; the referendum was held under military occupation with no credible independent monitoring. Russian-speaking Crimeans had genuinely mixed views — some welcomed it, others did not.

The Donbas conflict (April 2014 onward) began as a separatist uprising with Russian support and escalated to direct Russian military involvement. The Minsk Agreements (2015) were signed, never implemented, and are now moot.

## What This Means for Briefings

- The war is not intelligible as "unprovoked aggression" alone or as "defensive response to NATO" alone. Both frames suppress important evidence.
- Statements from Kyiv and Moscow should be understood in light of their respective historical frames, not just their immediate political incentives.
- Peace negotiations, if they resume, will founder on the civilizational question before they get to borders. Territory is a proxy for a deeper dispute about identity.
